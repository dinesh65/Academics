From Visible Cone
to Invisible Cone

One folded sheet of paper, and a shape that quietly measures what we cannot reach.

Dinesh Kumar · June 2026 · 20 min read

I was sitting at my study desk one evening, preparing to teach the lateral surface area of a cone to a class of 9th-grade students the next morning. I knew the formula — πrl — and I had the textbook diagram. But I did not really understand it. Not the formula. The thing the formula was talking about.

So I did what any mathematician should do more often: I picked up a piece of paper, cut out a sector, curled it, and taped the edges. A cone appeared in my hands.

What happened next took about forty minutes and quietly connected me to roughly two thousand years of human thought.

A cone is one of the most generative objects in mathematics. Not because it is complicated, but because it is the right shape to encode many things — and to hide them just well enough that finding them feels like genuine discovery.

This is the story of what I found that evening. I will try to write it the way I experienced it — one surprise at a time — until the small paper model on my desk reaches all the way to the large-scale structure of the cosmos.

I. The Paper Cone and Its Four Secrets

I took a circular disc of paper and cut out a sector — a wedge-shaped piece, the way you might cut a slice from a pie. I curled the remaining piece until its two straight edges met. A cone appeared.

Aha moment · 1

A cone is born from a circular sector. The straight edges of the sector become the slant edge. The arc of the sector becomes the base circle. The cone is a rolled-up sector — and understanding the cone is the same as understanding what happens when you roll up part of a circle. Most students never see this. They are given the formula and asked to use it.

I looked at the leftover wedge I had set aside. On a quiet impulse, I curled that too. It also became a cone — steeper, with a smaller base circle.

Aha moment · 2

Two cones from one disc. They are complementary: the wider sector makes a wider, flatter cone; the narrower wedge makes a taller, narrower one. The full disc is shared between them, and each remembers exactly which part of the disc it came from.

Now I held the two cones side by side and noticed something the textbook had never made visible. The base radius r was not something I had chosen independently — it was determined by which sector I had used. The cone was keeping a record of its own origin.

Aha moment · 3

The base radius r is not a free parameter. Once you fix the slant height l (the radius of the original disc) and the sector angle θ, the base radius is completely determined. There is no freedom left. The cone encodes its own history in its proportions.

The fourth realisation came when I thought about area. The sector of the original disc, when curled into a cone, does not change its area — it is the same piece of paper. So the lateral surface area of the cone equals the area of the sector. A sector of angle θ (out of 360°) from a disc of radius l has area:

Sector area  =  θ360° · πl² fraction of full disc × disc area
Arc length  =  θ360° · 2πl this arc becomes the base circle
So:  θ360° · 2πl = 2πr arc length = base circumference
⟹  θ360° = rl the key ratio
⟹  Lateral area = rl · πl² = πrl the formula, derived from first principles
Aha moment · 4

The formula πrl is not a definition to memorize. It is a natural consequence of the geometry of unrolling. The practical reason to use it — rather than the sector form — is that r and l can both be measured directly on a finished cone, whereas the sector angle θ cannot. Once the paper is curled, θ hides inside the shape. The formula is better because it uses what the object is willing to show you.

II. The Cone Remembers Its Past

The sector angle θ is now hidden inside the cone. Once the paper is curled and taped, you cannot see it or measure it directly with a protractor without unfolding everything again.

Yet the simple relationship we discovered earlier — θ360° = rl — still allows us to recover θ using only two measurements we can make from the outside: the base radius r and the slant height l.

It feels natural to express this in radians. A full circle is 360°, and the same full turn measured in radians is 2π. Replacing 360° with 2π in the earlier equation gives us:

θ360° = rl the key ratio from Section I
⟹  θ (rad)2π = rl replacing 360° by 2π
⟹  θ = 2πrl the sector angle in radians

Visualising it brings a quiet thrill: when the cone is standing in front of you, its base circumference (2πr) divided by the slant height (l) directly gives the sector angle θ in radians. The three-dimensional cone quietly hands back the two-dimensional angle that was lost when the paper was curled. This is not a new discovery — it is the very definition of a radian, playing out beautifully on the physical object we just made.

The angle that disappeared when I curled the paper can be recovered from two simple measurements made on the outside of the finished cone — without ever looking inside.

There is also a clean geometric view. Slice the cone through its apex and axis, and you get a flat triangle with apex angle 2α, plainly visible with nothing more than a protractor. Then, from the right triangle formed by the axis, radius, and slant height:

sin α = rl the apex half-angle of the cone
⟹  θ = 2π · sin α   (in radians) sector angle is 2π times the sine of the apex half-angle

A hidden quantity recovered from accessible surface measurements through a known relationship — and the same hidden angle has more than one way back into view.

III. The Invisible Cone — How We See the World

When you look at an object — a tree, a star, the Moon — light rays travel from every point on the object to your eye. All those rays together form a cone in space, with you at the apex and the object at the base. This cone is completely invisible. There is no paper, no tape. Yet it obeys exactly the same geometry as the one I made on my desk.

For this invisible cone the same relationship holds: sin α = r/l. What the eye (or a telescope) directly registers is the angle α — how wide the object appears from where we stand. What we usually want to know, but cannot measure by simply looking, are the object's actual physical size r and its distance l. The cone formula connects all three: knowing any two gives you the third.

Now ask: what happens as the object moves farther away? As it recedes, the slant distance l and the straight-line distance d become almost the same. The cone becomes sharper and α approaches zero. For small angles (in radians), sin α ≈ α itself, so the formula simplifies beautifully:

α ≈ rd the small-angle formula; α in radians, valid when α ≪ 1
Aha moment · 5

When the object is far away, α becomes tiny. For such small angles in radians, sin α, tan α, and α itself are all practically equal. This is the small-angle formula: α ≈ r/d. It is the geometric backbone of all astronomical measurement — every telescope observation, every stellar radius, every distance we calculate in the sky comes through this relationship. It all begins from asking what happens when our paper cone becomes a needle.

IV. The Greeks Already Knew — They Just Used Shadows

The invisible cone of sight is ancient. Greek mathematicians used cone geometry to measure things no one had ever reached, using nothing more sophisticated than sticks, shadows, and ingenuity. What makes their work remarkable is not just the answers they obtained, but the structure of their reasoning: each one identified a hidden quantity, found accessible measurements, and used a geometric relationship.

Eratosthenes and the Earth (≈ 240 BC)

On the summer solstice, the Sun was directly overhead at Syene (modern Aswan): no shadow at noon, and the Sun's reflection visible at the bottom of a deep well. On the same day in Alexandria, a vertical gnomon cast a shadow corresponding to an angle of 7.2° — exactly one-fiftieth of a full circle. If the Sun's rays arrive parallel (they very nearly do, given the enormous distance of the Sun), that angle equals the central angle subtended by the arc between the two cities.*

7.2° = 150 × 360° fraction of full circle subtended at Earth's centre
Circumference = 50 × 800 km = 40,000 km modern value: 40,075 km

One shadow. One angle. One already-known distance. The circumference of the Earth, recovered from accessible measurements through a geometric relationship. The angle, at least, needed nothing beyond a stick and a city Eratosthenes never had to leave.†

The cone implicit in Eratosthenes' reasoning: the Sun's rays form a cone whose apex is at the Sun and whose axis passes through the Earth's centre. Eratosthenes was reading the half-angle of that cone from a shadow cast on the ground.

Aristarchus and the Moon (≈ 270 BC)

During a lunar eclipse, the Earth blocks the Sun's light, casting a conical shadow — the umbra — behind it into space. That shadow is genuinely cone-shaped, tapering from Earth's width down to a point far behind it. The Moon, as it crosses through this shadow, spends a measurable amount of time inside it. By comparing that crossing time to the Moon's own apparent diameter in the sky, Aristarchus could infer how wide the shadow was at the Moon's distance — and from that, how far away the Moon was. The shadow he reasoned about was a real, physical cone of darkness; the geometry he actually wrote down, in his surviving treatise[3], worked with its flat cross-section — circles and similar triangles in the eclipse plane.

Aristarchus pioneered this geometric approach using eclipse timings. His method yielded Moon's distance of an estimate 60 Earth-radii. The modern value is 60.3. He was within roughly 0.5% of the correct answer, measuring from the ground, more than two millennia ago, using a cone he could never see but whose geometry he could reason about exactly.

The Earth's shadow is a cone. The Moon's path through it is a chord across that cone's cross-section. Aristarchus read the Moon's distance from the shadow of the Earth — recovering a hidden quantity from accessible measurements through the geometry of a cone.

The recurring pattern

In every case we have seen — the paper disc, the visual cone, Eratosthenes' shadow, Aristarchus' eclipse — a hidden quantity is recovered from accessible measurements through a known geometric relationship. The cone is not merely a shape. It is a template for inference: a way of using what you can see to find what you cannot.

V. Other Directions Worth Following

The paper cone I made that evening turned out to be a single entry point into a much larger landscape. A few threads worth pulling, for anyone who wants to keep going past where this essay stops.

Conic sections

We built a cone and asked what it looks like unrolled. The classical question runs the other way: what curve do you get when you slice a cone with a plane? Depending on the angle of the cut, the answer is a circle, an ellipse, a parabola, or a hyperbola — the four conic sections, studied systematically by Apollonius of Perga[4] two thousand years before they turned out to also describe planetary orbits, the path of a thrown ball, and the focusing surface of a satellite dish. The cone is generous twice over: once in how it unrolls, and once in how it slices.

A puzzle to sit with: Take a cone and cut it with a plane that does not pass through the apex, at some angle to the base. The boundary of the cut is an ellipse sitting tilted in space. Now ask: where, inside that cone, do you have to place a sphere so that it touches the cone's slant surface all the way around in a circle, and just touches the cutting plane at a single point? The two such spheres (one on each side of the cutting plane) touch the ellipse at its two foci — the same foci you'd mark with two pins and a loop of string if you were drawing the ellipse directly. It is a satisfying exercise to work out why, and it is exactly the kind of thing the paper cone on your desk is sturdy enough to help you see.

Other hidden cones

Once you start looking, cones of inference show up well beyond shadows and eclipses. A camera's field of view is a cone, and so is a flashlight's beam, a loudspeaker's sound dispersion, and the spray pattern of a nozzle — in every case, a hidden quantity (distance, intensity, coverage) is recoverable from an angle and a known relationship, the same template this essay has been tracing.

Even more striking is the Mach cone. An object moving faster than sound drags a conical shock wave whose half-angle α satisfies sin α= vsoundvobject. A single Schlieren photograph of the cone (or the vapour cone around a supersonic aircraft) immediately reveals the object’s speed — another elegant instance of an angle standing in for a quantity we cannot measure directly.

Cones already built that way

Many cones in nature and engineering are not accidental — they are deliberately or evolutionarily shaped for a clear purpose. Conifer trees (such as pines and firs) have cone-shaped canopies that help them shed snow easily, resist strong winds, and allow sunlight to reach the lower branches. Young coast redwoods — the tallest trees in the world — show this conical form most beautifully.

A fountain pen nib tapers conically to concentrate pressure at the tip and draw ink smoothly by capillary action. Rocket nose cones are shaped this way to reduce air resistance at high speeds. A sharper angle cuts through the air more efficiently, though engineers must balance it with the need for strength and payload space.

VI. What This Means for Teaching

I want to end where I began — at the study desk the evening before class.

The discoveries I made with that paper disc were not new mathematics. Every one of them was already known. What was new was the quiet sequence of surprise — each one arriving as the answer to a question I was already holding in my mind.

That sequence is teachable. Not the specific discoveries — different students will find different things. But the posture is teachable: picking up the object, refusing to accept a formula until you feel why it must be true, and following each surprise wherever it leads.

The cone does not reveal all its secrets at once. It rewards patience and attention. First it shows you a formula (the paper, the sector, the area). Then it shows you a hidden angle (recoverable from the surface). Then it shows you an invisible cone of sight. And if you keep following the thread, it quietly takes you from your desk to the Moon, to the stars, and to the edge of spacetime.

The visible cone becomes the invisible cone. And the invisible cone, it turns out, is more powerful and structurally important than anything you can hold in your hands.

FINAL THOUGHT

The teacher who finds a way to lead students from the visible to the invisible is teaching geometry in its deepest sense — not as a collection of formulas, but as a language in which the universe writes about itself.

Mathematics is not confined to mental exercise or the play of symbols. It can be felt in the heart — and that feeling is often what carries the mind toward its most wonderful mathemtics.

He is working at the intersection of networked dynamical systems, spectral graph theory, and mathematical biology, with research extending into reaction–diffusion systems and bioeconomic optimal control.
